# Ciel & Stone — Conversion, SEO & Content Strategy

Strategic restructure of the homepage and site. Copy, architecture, and offer system only. Visual design is reserved for the next pass.

---

## A. Strategic Diagnosis

### What the site is doing well

Before the critique: the site is not broken. It is tasteful, coherent, and the Feasibility Read is a genuinely differentiated lead-capture mechanism that most design-build studios do not have. The Next.js structure is clean, metadata exists on every route, and the project/experience pages are well-sequenced. The problem is not craft. The problem is that the craft is aimed at the wrong targets.

### What is limiting conversion

**The hero asks visitors to choose, not to act.** Three CTAs sit side by side — *Start Your Project*, *View Portfolio*, *Email the Studio*. Three equal options is indecision architecture. A serious prospect decides in under four seconds whether they belong here, and a tie between three paths pushes them into "View Portfolio" (the path of least commitment), which is where leads go to die.

**The strongest offer is buried.** The Feasibility Read is the most specific, most premium, most conversion-ready product on the entire site. It has a price anchor built in (the fee credits against preconstruction for priority fits), it is an actual deliverable, and it is unique to the brand. It sits at the bottom of the homepage behind Philosophy, Process, Preconstruction, Services, Trust, and Featured Work. A visitor who bounces at 40% scroll never sees it.

**There is no social proof on the site. Anywhere.** No testimonials. No stats. No press. No "completed X projects" counter. No partner or trade logos. No architect collaborators named. No AIA, NKBA, NARI, or similar credentials. No client names or project attribution. For a premium price point ($50k to $500k+), this is the single largest trust gap. Luxury is not a font — it is risk reduction. There is currently nothing on the site that reduces the risk of sending a down payment to a studio a homeowner has never met.

**The copy leads with philosophy instead of outcome.** The opening section after the hero is titled "Philosophy" and says *"Design comes first, because the best construction decisions are made before work begins."* This is a thesis statement, not a hook. A serious homeowner who just landed from an Instagram reel does not need your philosophy before they know whether you work on their kind of project, in their market, at their budget. Philosophy is a final-stage sale, not a first-stage one.

**Two sections (Philosophy and Trust) say the same thing with different words.** Both describe restraint, attentiveness, calm professionalism, and clarity on scope and cost. The reader gets no new information on the second pass — they get a sense that the site is running out of things to say.

**The hero headline is interchangeable.** *"Thoughtful design. Precise execution."* could sit on the website of a dentist, an architect, a furniture maker, a watch brand, or a marketing agency. It does not say what Ciel & Stone builds, for whom, or in what market.

**The subheadline uses "clarity" twice in two sentences.** *"clarity before construction... a calm path from concept to completion."* The word is a crutch; it appears again in the info boxes and again in the philosophy section. Clarity is the brand's best value promise, which means it should be *demonstrated*, not *repeated*.

**There is no price anchor anywhere visible.** "$50k to $500k+" exists in a small info chip in the hero but is never restated or framed. A homeowner landing from a cost-breakdown reel wants to know within 10 seconds whether their $180k kitchen is in scope. Right now the answer is buried and ambiguous.

**Service "cards" are not services.** Design-Build, Consultation, and Project Planning are described in one sentence each, lead nowhere, and do not have dedicated pages. There is no way for a homeowner to self-identify into "I'm looking for a bathroom remodel" — which is what 70% of their organic search traffic will actually want.

### What is limiting SEO

**No service landing pages.** The site has no page that could ever rank for `kitchen remodel los angeles`, `home addition seattle`, `adu builder portland`, `whole home renovation pacific northwest`, or any of the transactional phrases that a qualified homeowner types. Google indexes what it can find, and right now it can find four pages: home, process, contact, feasibility, and a small set of project detail pages. The site is essentially invisible to search.

**No market/location pages.** The footer says "Los Angeles / Pacific Northwest" but there is no `/los-angeles`, no `/seattle`, no `/portland`. For local service businesses, city pages are the single highest-ROI SEO investment. They are currently absent.

**No cost or investment content.** "How much does a kitchen remodel cost in LA" is one of the highest-intent residential search queries in the country, and a luxury design-build studio is the exact right answer to it. There is no page on the site that addresses it.

**No editorial or insight content.** A studio that sells itself on "thinking before building" has published zero thinking. This is the easiest SEO deficit to fix and one of the most on-brand.

**Thin metadata reuse.** Several pages share nearly identical descriptions ("residential design-build and preconstruction studio..."). Google dedupes. Every page needs a distinct meta description that reflects the searcher's actual intent on that page.

**OG image is a placeholder SVG.** Every social share of the site right now renders as a generic gray rectangle with no branding. This is the easiest credibility leak on the site.

### What is limiting social performance

**No landing page is built for cold social traffic.** A visitor who clicks a bio link after watching a before/after reel has completely different expectations than a visitor who typed the URL. They want, in order: recognition ("this matches what I just saw"), proof ("they've actually done this"), next step ("what happens if I reach out"). The current homepage delivers philosophy first.

**No page is built around "before/after" or visual storytelling.** The portfolio is strong but abstract — "Story, clarity, and luxury detail." A social visitor wants *specific* transformation. Square footage, cost range, timeline, before photo, after photo, what changed structurally. That page does not exist.

**No page addresses the homeowner's *real* first question.** The real first question from a $200k-remodel prospect who clicked a reel is "what would this cost me" or "how long would it take" or "can you even do my kind of project." The site currently answers "design comes first." Correct philosophy. Wrong order.

### What is limiting luxury credibility

**Luxury credibility comes from three signals**: who you've worked with, who has written about you, and how specifically you describe the work you've done. The site currently carries none of these. The philosophy copy implies quality without ever naming a single collaborating architect, photographer, landscape designer, millworker, or publication. In a market where Remodelista, Dwell, Architectural Digest, and Instagram collaborations are how homeowners vet firms, absence of name-drops reads as inexperience, not discretion.

## B. New Homepage Strategy

### What the homepage should do

One job, ranked: **convert a qualified homeowner into a Feasibility Read booking or studio inquiry within a single scroll.** Every section earns its place against that outcome.

### Priority order of messaging

1. **Recognition** — within three seconds the visitor must know this is for them: residential, design-build, higher-end, preconstruction-led, LA + Pacific Northwest.
2. **Credibility** — within ten seconds the visitor must see at least one proof signal: a stat, a client voice, a publication, a named collaborator, a completed-projects count.
3. **Specificity** — project types, price ranges, timelines are shown plainly. No guessing.
4. **Offer clarity** — the Feasibility Read is promoted to the top half of the page, not the bottom. It is framed as the *intelligent first step*, not the last resort.
5. **Process confidence** — the process is present but condensed; its job is to say "we've done this enough to systematize it," not to teach.
6. **Work** — featured projects function as visual proof, not as a portfolio browse.
7. **Trust cues** — testimonials, credentials, collaborators. Added. Non-optional.
8. **Action** — single primary CTA, repeated, with one secondary softer path.

### What belongs on the homepage vs elsewhere

**Homepage only**: hero, proof strip, services overview (as outcome bundles), Feasibility Read, condensed process, featured work, testimonials, FAQ, final CTA.

**Dedicated landing pages (build next)**: each service (kitchens, bathrooms, additions, ADUs/guest houses, whole-home, outdoor living), each market (LA, Portland, Seattle), the Feasibility Read as a standalone product page, an investment / cost primer, an insights/editorial index.

**Future articles (SEO)**: cost breakdowns, process explainers, material guides, timeline expectations, permit/zoning primers per market.

### Emotional journey

Visitor arrives unsettled — they've been thinking about this remodel for months, they've gotten one bad quote already, they don't know who to trust. The homepage should walk them through four states:

1. *"This is the right kind of firm for me."* (Recognition)
2. *"They've actually done this."* (Credibility)
3. *"I can see what working with them looks like."* (Specificity)
4. *"The next step is low-risk and intelligent."* (Action)

The Feasibility Read is the answer to state 4. It is not free, which is *part* of its luxury signal. It is structured, it is a deliverable, and it is the intelligent first step. That is the entire conversion thesis of the site.

## C. Proposed Homepage Architecture

A single-scroll, eleven-section homepage. Each section has a single job.

### 1. Hero

**Purpose:** Recognition + primary CTA in the first viewport.
**Addresses:** "Is this site for me?"
**CTA:** Single primary — *Begin a Feasibility Read*. Single secondary — *See recent work*.
**Content:** One headline, one subheadline, two buttons, one small anchor strip (markets + price range + project types) beneath the fold line.

### 2. Proof Strip

**Purpose:** Instant credibility before the visitor scrolls past the fold.
**Addresses:** "Are these people real?"
**CTA:** None.
**Content:** A horizontal band with three to five proof elements: completed projects count, cities served, featured-in publications (if any), collaborator logos (architects, photographers, makers), or — in the interim while those assets are gathered — three short quoted lines from clients with first name + city + project type.

### 3. Who This Is For

**Purpose:** Self-qualification. Cold traffic needs to know in plain language whether they belong here.
**Addresses:** Fit. Price anchor. Scope anchor.
**CTA:** None (this is a filter, not a closer).
**Content:** A short three-column "we work best with..." block naming project types, investment ranges, and the kind of client this studio fits. Frank, unapologetic. Filters out unserious traffic.

### 4. Services, Framed as Outcomes

**Purpose:** Let the homeowner self-identify into their project type.
**Addresses:** "What exactly do you build?"
**CTA:** Each card links to its own service landing page (built in phase 2).
**Content:** Six outcome-led cards — Kitchens, Bathrooms & Primary Suites, Additions, ADUs & Guest Houses, Whole-Home Renovations, Outdoor Living. Each card: one-line outcome, typical investment range, typical timeline, link to the page.

### 5. The Feasibility Read *(promoted)*

**Purpose:** Convert the qualified visitor. This is the site's primary conversion event.
**Addresses:** "What does the first step actually look like?"
**CTA:** *Begin a Feasibility Read* (primary, repeated).
**Content:** Explain the deliverable clearly — eight questions, a written read covering scope bracket, investment range, preconstruction plan, and day-one risk flags. Name the fee. Name the credit-back policy. Name the turnaround time. This is the one section that should feel almost product-page in its clarity.

### 6. Process (condensed)

**Purpose:** Show that the studio operates a system, not improvisation.
**Addresses:** "Will this project be chaos?"
**CTA:** Link to `/process` for the full multi-step page.
**Content:** Four phases, each with a one-line outcome (not a philosophical description). The current process page is good; the homepage version should compress it.

### 7. Featured Work

**Purpose:** Visual proof.
**Addresses:** "Does the work look like what I want?"
**CTA:** *See all projects* → `/projects`.
**Content:** Three featured projects in the existing carousel. Each card should now include: location, project type, square footage, year — so the visitor gets context without clicking through.

### 8. Client Voice

**Purpose:** Risk reduction through specific, attributable praise.
**Addresses:** "Will the experience be worth it?"
**CTA:** None.
**Content:** Two or three full testimonials. Attribution required — first name, last initial, city, project type, year. A single anonymous "a recent client said" undermines the premium. If there are no testimonials yet, this section should be built as soon as the first project wraps, and in the interim it can be replaced with a short "studio principles" block that reads as a direct commitment (not philosophy — commitment).

### 9. Investment & Timeline Primer

**Purpose:** Address the biggest unasked question on the site.
**Addresses:** "What will this cost me and how long will it take?"
**CTA:** *See full investment guide* → `/investment` (built in phase 2).
**Content:** A small table or structured block with honest typical ranges for each project type. This is the single most SEO-valuable piece of copy on the homepage.

### 10. Frequently Asked

**Purpose:** Objection handling + SEO capture. Homeowners search these questions verbatim.
**Addresses:** Every remaining friction.
**CTA:** None.
**Content:** Five to seven questions with short, direct answers. FAQ schema markup for SEO.

### 11. Final CTA

**Purpose:** Close.
**Addresses:** "I'm ready — now what?"
**CTA:** *Begin a Feasibility Read* (primary) + *Email the studio* (secondary, soft).
**Content:** One sentence. One button. One softer alternative.

## E. Offer & CTA System

### The offer, framed clearly

There are three offers, arranged on a ladder from cold to committed. Each one has a specific price anchor, a specific deliverable, and a specific conversion rate target.

**Tier 1 — Feasibility Read (lead offer).** A flat studio fee. Eight questions. A written read delivered within 5 business days covering scope bracket, investment range, preconstruction plan, and day-one risk flags. For priority-fit engagements, the fee is credited against preconstruction. *This is the conversion goal for almost all qualified traffic.*

**Tier 2 — Preconstruction engagement.** The full planning phase: resolved scope, budget, design direction, material selections locked enough to move to fixed pricing. Sold as a defined scope-and-fee agreement, not a retainer. *This is where the Feasibility Read graduates qualified buyers.*

**Tier 3 — Design-Build engagement.** The full project, carried from preconstruction through completion by one accountable team. *This is the end-state revenue event.*

The consultation can remain as a softer entry for visitors who are not yet ready for a Feasibility Read — but it should be positioned as *"a 30-minute introductory call"*, not as a product. Making the consultation a serious offer competes with the Feasibility Read and dilutes both.

### The CTA system

**Primary CTA, sitewide:** `Begin a Feasibility Read →`
Appears in the hero, in the Feasibility Read section, in the final CTA block, and as the right-side button in the navigation bar. No variation in wording. Consistency compounds.

**Secondary CTA, sitewide:** `See recent work` or `Email the studio` depending on context.
The secondary is always softer, less committing, and never competes visually with the primary.

**Tertiary soft CTAs, for cold or editorial traffic:**
- `See what your project could cost` → `/investment`
- `Explore kitchen work` / `Explore additions` → service landing pages
- `Read our process` → `/process`

**Contextual CTAs by traffic source:**

| Traffic source | Likely intent | Primary CTA to surface |
|---|---|---|
| Cold Instagram/TikTok | Visual recognition | *See recent work* then surface Feasibility Read at scroll 60% |
| Bio link | Brand curiosity | Full homepage, standard primary |
| Before/after reel click | Transformation curiosity | Land on service page (e.g. kitchens) with Feasibility Read primary |
| Cost-breakdown post | Price research | Land on `/investment` with Feasibility Read primary |
| Educational reel | Process curiosity | Land on `/process` with Feasibility Read primary |
| Organic Google search | High intent (local + service) | Land on service × market page (e.g. `/kitchens/los-angeles`) with Feasibility Read primary |

**CTA language rules:**
- Lead verbs are action verbs with specific objects. *Begin a Feasibility Read* beats *Get Started*.
- Never use *Schedule*, *Book*, *Sign Up*, *Free*, *Learn More*, or *Submit*.
- Secondary CTAs can be softer in tone but never passive in verb.
- One primary per viewport. If two primaries compete, both lose.

## F. SEO & Social Expansion Plan

### The site architecture to build, in priority order

**Phase 2 — Service landing pages** (these earn qualified organic leads and are the single highest-ROI SEO investment)

- `/services/kitchens`
- `/services/bathrooms-primary-suites`
- `/services/additions`
- `/services/adus-guest-houses`
- `/services/whole-home-renovations`
- `/services/outdoor-living`

Each page follows the same template: headline + outcome summary, typical investment range, typical timeline, three to five featured projects of that type, a short "what's included in a kitchen engagement" block, a process recap, an FAQ specific to that service, and the Feasibility Read CTA.

**Phase 2 — Market landing pages** (local SEO)

- `/los-angeles` or `/markets/los-angeles`
- `/portland`
- `/seattle`

Each page is anchored to the city: neighborhoods served, typical project types in that market, local permitting context, a few featured projects from that market, local investment ranges, and a Feasibility Read CTA.

**Phase 2 — Service × market pages** (the real organic traffic engine)

Once the base service and market pages exist, combine them:
- `/kitchens/los-angeles`
- `/kitchens/portland`
- `/additions/seattle`
- `/adus/los-angeles`
- etc.

These intersections rank because they match exactly how homeowners search: *kitchen remodel los angeles*, *adu builder portland*, *home addition seattle cost*. Build the top 6 to 10 of these over phase 2.

**Phase 2 — Supporting pages**

- `/feasibility` — already exists. Expand into a full product page with deliverable details, pricing, and examples.
- `/investment` — a long-form cost primer. High SEO value and high trust value.
- `/about` — studio story, named collaborators, principles-as-commitments, photography. Currently missing.
- `/insights` — editorial index.

**Phase 3 — Editorial content (/insights)**

The ten articles, in priority order, that match both luxury positioning and homeowner search behavior:

1. *What a kitchen renovation actually costs in Los Angeles (honestly).*
2. *How to read a contractor's estimate without getting burned.*
3. *The five decisions that blow a renovation budget.*
4. *ADU vs. addition in California: what actually pencils out.*

### Social traffic readiness

Changes the homepage and site architecture need to absorb cold social traffic:

1. **A hero that matches the reel.** Social visitors come from specific content. The homepage cannot match every reel, but service and market landing pages can. Each Instagram reel or TikTok should link to the most relevant landing page, not the homepage.
2. **A visible proof strip above the fold.** A social visitor will not scroll looking for credibility. If the proof isn't in the first viewport, it isn't seen.
3. **A "what does this cost" answer within two scrolls.** The Investment section (section 9) handles this on the homepage. The `/investment` page handles it for direct social links.
4. **Before/after landing pages for reel traffic.** For every before/after reel that performs, a dedicated case-study page that expands the story with the full numbers: cost, timeline, square footage, permits, challenges, outcome. Link the reel's bio to the case study, not the homepage.
5. **A "Start with a Feasibility Read" framing on every soft CTA.** Social traffic shouldn't see "Email us" as the first option — they should see the product-shaped offer.

## G. Notes for the Design Phase

Hand these to Claude Design when the structure above is in. Design's job is to lift the copy, not replace the message.

**Hierarchy and restraint.** The current design is already calm, which is correct. The next pass should strengthen hierarchy: one dominant type size per section, one dominant color, generous negative space. Luxury visuals come from ratio and restraint, not from saturation.

**Photography is the brand.** Commission or curate serious residential photography — wide, warm, architectural. Stock imagery will kill the brand faster than any copy mistake. Every featured project card, every service card, every market page needs its own photography. Placeholder SVGs are the current OG image — that needs to change before the next Instagram share.

**Typography.** A single editorial serif for headlines. A clean, slightly wide sans for body and UI. No more than two typefaces. No display script anywhere.

**Color.** The existing restraint is correct. Consider earning one accent color (ink, bronze, or warm clay) that appears only on primary CTAs and nowhere else. Scarcity gives it meaning.

**Proof strip treatment.** The proof strip in section 2 will make or break the homepage. It should be quietly confident — small type, spacious kerning, attribution in italics. Do not style it like a marketing band.

**CTAs.** The primary CTA button should be the single most visually committed element on the page. Solid fill, warm accent, slight weight. Every secondary CTA is a ghost or text link. Visual hierarchy should enforce "one primary per viewport."

**Forms.** The contact and feasibility forms are acceptable but can feel more premium with: fewer visible fields per step, editorial question phrasing ("What's the home like today?" beats "Project Notes"), and a confirmation screen that reads as personal rather than transactional.

**Motion.** The Lenis scroll and narrative sequences are tasteful. Keep them. Do not add more motion. Add restraint.

**Featured project cards.** Each card should now surface: photo, project name, city, project type, year, square footage — visible without hover. The current abstract captions get replaced with factual ones.

**FAQ schema.** Implement FAQ schema markup on the homepage FAQ and on every service page FAQ. This is a structural-SEO task, not visual, but flag it now so the design phase doesn't disturb it.

**Breadcrumbs and internal linking.** As service × market pages are added, breadcrumbs and related-page rails become structurally important. Factor in navigation that can support the eventual 30–50 page site, not the current 6-page site.

**OG images.** Every primary page needs a real OG image. The generic placeholder is the single easiest credibility fix on the site.

The current site is a tasteful brochure. The restructured site is a conversion-ready studio destination with a clear product-shaped lead offer, outcome-led services, honest price anchors, real proof, and an architecture built to absorb both paid social traffic and organic search over the next year.

The execution moves from abstract to specific, from philosophy to outcome, from three competing CTAs to one disciplined primary. Ciel & Stone stops sounding like a firm with good taste and starts sounding like a firm that takes on serious residential projects and finishes them well.
